# Florida First Appearance Hearings: What You Need to Know

If your loved one has been arrested in Florida, their **First Appearance Hearing** is one of the most critical moments in the criminal justice process. It is the first time they will stand before a judge, and it is when the judge decides whether they can be released on bail and under what conditions.

At Shamrock Bail Bonds, we know that time is of the essence. This guide explains exactly what happens at a First Appearance, what it is *not*, and provides live schedules and Zoom links for counties across Florida so you can stay informed.

## What is a First Appearance Hearing?

Under Florida Rule of Criminal Procedure 3.130, anyone who is arrested and not immediately released must be brought before a judge within **24 hours** of their arrest. This proceeding is known as the First Appearance.

During this hearing, the judge will perform several essential functions:
1. **Inform the Defendant of the Charges:** The judge will state the specific crimes the defendant is accused of committing.
2. **Advise of Constitutional Rights:** The judge will remind the defendant of their right to remain silent and their right to an attorney.
3. **Determine Probable Cause:** The judge will review the arrest report to ensure law enforcement had a valid legal reason (probable cause) to make the arrest. If there is no probable cause, the defendant must be released.
4. **Set Bail and Conditions of Release:** This is the most crucial step for families. The judge will decide if the defendant is eligible for bail, set the bond amount, and establish any conditions of release (such as a no-contact order or GPS monitoring).
5. **Appoint Counsel:** If the defendant cannot afford an attorney, the judge will appoint a Public Defender.

## What a First Appearance is NOT

It is vital to understand the limitations of a First Appearance:
* **It is NOT a trial:** The judge will not decide guilt or innocence.
* **It is NOT the time to argue the facts:** Defendants should *never* try to explain their side of the story or argue with the judge during this hearing. Anything said is recorded and can be used against them later.
* **It is NOT an Arraignment:** An arraignment is a separate, later hearing where the defendant formally enters a plea of "Guilty," "Not Guilty," or "No Contest."

## How Bail is Determined

When setting bail, Florida judges consider several factors to ensure the defendant returns for future court dates and does not pose a threat to the community. These factors include:
* The nature and severity of the charges.
* The defendant's past criminal record and history of appearing in court.
* The defendant's ties to the community (family, employment, length of residence).
* The potential danger the defendant poses to the community.
