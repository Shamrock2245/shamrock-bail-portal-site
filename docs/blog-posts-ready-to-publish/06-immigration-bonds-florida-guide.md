## Immigration Detention and Bail: How It's Different

When a person is detained by U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), the standard Florida state criminal bail system does not apply. Immigration detention and criminal detention are **two separate legal systems**.

Key differences:

| | Criminal Bail (State) | Immigration Bond (Federal) |
|---|---|---|
| **Set by** | Florida state judge | ICE officer or immigration judge |
| **Court** | Lee County/Florida state courts | U.S. Immigration Court (EOIR) |
| **Bond type** | Surety bond via state-licensed bondsman | Delivery bond or departure bond |
| **Average amount** | $500 – $50,000 | $1,500 – $25,000+ |
| **Payment** | 10% premium via bondsman | Cash or surety bond (10%) |

## What Is an Immigration Delivery Bond?

A **delivery bond** is the most common type of immigration bond. It allows a detained non-citizen to be released from ICE detention while their immigration case is pending in immigration court.

To be eligible for a delivery bond:
- ICE must determine the person is eligible for release
- The person must receive an **I-200 (Warrant for Arrest)** or **I-286 (Notice of Custody Determination)**
- A bond amount must be set (either by ICE or an immigration judge)

---

## What Is an Immigration Departure Bond?

A **departure bond** guarantees that the person will voluntarily leave the United States when required. If they depart voluntarily, the bond is returned. If they fail to depart, the bond is forfeited.

Departure bonds are less common and typically used in cases where the individual agrees to voluntary departure as part of their immigration resolution.

## How Much Do Immigration Bonds Cost in Florida?

The minimum immigration bond under federal law is **$1,500**. Most bonds fall in the range of $1,500 to $10,000, though bonds can be set much higher depending on:

- Criminal history
- Prior immigration violations
- Flight risk assessment by ICE
- Length of time in the U.S. and community ties

**Shamrock's premium** for immigration bonds is 15% of the bond amount — slightly higher than state criminal bonds because immigration bonds are federal and carry additional administrative complexity.

**Example:** A $5,000 immigration bond = $750 premium paid to Shamrock.

The alternative — paying the full bond amount in cash directly to ICE — is also an option but requires the full amount upfront, which most families cannot afford.

## How to Get Someone Released from ICE Detention — Step by Step

### Step 1: Find Out Where They Are Held
The ICE detainee locator at [locator.ice.gov](https://locator.ice.gov) allows you to search by name, date of birth, and country of birth. Alternatively, call Shamrock — we can assist with the search.

### Step 2: Verify Bond Eligibility
Not all detainees are eligible for a bond. ICE may issue a no-bond determination for individuals with certain criminal convictions or prior deportation orders. If bond is denied, an immigration attorney can request a bond hearing before an immigration judge.

### Step 3: Get the Bond Amount
Once bond is set — either by ICE (on the I-286) or by an immigration judge — contact Shamrock with the bond amount and the facility location.

### Step 4: Sign the Bond Agreement
Shamrock sends the bond agreement to you electronically. The **Obligor** (the person posting the immigration bond) must sign the federal Form I-352 (Immigration Bond) in addition to our standard agreement.

### Step 5: Bond Posted — Release Within 24 to 48 Hours
Once the immigration bond is posted with ICE, the detainee is released — typically within 24 to 48 hours. This timeline is set by ICE processing, not by the bondsman.

## Can ICE Detainees Be Released on Their Own Recognizance?

Yes, in some cases. An immigration judge may release a detainee on an **Order of Supervision** without requiring a bond — similar to a PR bond in criminal court. This is more common for individuals with strong community ties, no criminal history, and a pending immigration case with a realistic chance of relief.

An experienced immigration attorney is essential in advocating for this outcome.

## FAQ: Immigration Bonds in Florida

**Q: Does Shamrock provide bilingual (Spanish) service?**  
Yes. Call our Spanish line at **(239) 955-0301**. Our team is fully bilingual.

**Q: Can the immigration bond co-signer be undocumented?**  
No. The person signing the immigration bond (obligor) must be a U.S. citizen or lawful permanent resident.

**Q: What happens if the detained person misses their immigration court date?**  
The immigration bond is forfeited and ICE will issue an order of deportation. The obligor (co-signer) loses the bond premium and may be pursued for the full bond amount.

**Q: Does paying an immigration bond affect the immigration case outcome?**  
No. Posting an immigration bond simply secures release during the pending case. It does not affect the merits of the immigration case or grant any legal status.

**Q: Can a criminal conviction prevent immigration bond release?**  
Yes. Certain conviction categories — aggravated felonies, crimes of moral turpitude — can make a person ineligible for bond under federal immigration law.
